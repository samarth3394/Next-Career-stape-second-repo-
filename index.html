Common Questions

Frequently asked questions

Quick answers to the most common questions Indian students ask about studying in Germany.

Is education really free in Germany for Indian students? ▼
Yes! Most public universities in Germany do not charge tuition fees for master's programs — this applies to all students regardless of nationality. You only pay a small semester contribution of around €150–350 per semester, which usually includes a public transport pass. However, you need to cover your living expenses, which are approximately €934 per month (the blocked account requirement as of 2024).
Do I need to know German to study in Germany? ▼
Not necessarily. There are over 1,600 master's programs taught entirely in English in Germany. For these programs, you need IELTS (6.0–6.5) or TOEFL scores. However, learning basic German (A1–A2) is highly recommended for daily life — grocery shopping, dealing with authorities, and socialising. For German-taught programs, you will need a B2 or C1 level certificate (TestDaF or DSH).
What is a blocked account and how much money do I need? ▼
A blocked account (Sperrkonto) is a special bank account required for your German student visa. It proves that you have enough money to support yourself for one year. As of 2024, you need to deposit approximately €11,208 (€934 per month × 12 months). You can withdraw a fixed amount each month after arriving in Germany. Providers like Expatrio, Fintiba, and Deutsche Bank offer blocked accounts for Indian students.
Can I work while studying in Germany? ▼
Yes! International students in Germany are allowed to work up to 120 full days or 240 half days per year without needing a separate work permit. Many students work as student assistants (HiWi) at universities, part-time in companies, or in restaurants and retail. The average student job pays €12–€15 per hour. This income can significantly help cover your living expenses.
What happens after I complete my degree? ▼
After completing your master's degree, Germany gives you an 18-month post-study job seeker visa. During this time, you can work in any job to support yourself while looking for a position related to your qualification. Once you find a job matching your degree, you can switch to a work visa (EU Blue Card). After working for about 21–33 months, you can apply for permanent residency. Germany actively wants skilled graduates to stay.

Germany Information

Everything you need to know about studying in Germany

A complete guide for Indian students planning to pursue their Masters in Germany. From university types to PR — all your questions answered in one place.

🇩🇪 Why Study in Germany?

Germany has become one of the most popular study destinations for Indian students — and for good reason. With nearly zero tuition fees at public universities, world-class education quality, a booming job market, and a clear path to permanent residency, Germany offers everything an ambitious student needs. Over 40,000 Indian students are currently studying in Germany, making it the third largest group of international students in the country. Whether you are an engineering graduate, a business student, or from a science background, Germany has programs that match your career goals.

🏛️ Types of Universities in Germany

Germany has two main types of universities, and understanding the difference is important for choosing the right one for you.

Technical Universities (Technische Universität — TU)

These are traditional research-oriented universities that offer a wide range of subjects including engineering, natural sciences, humanities, and social sciences. They focus heavily on academic research, theory, and innovation. Famous examples include TU Munich, RWTH Aachen, TU Berlin, and KIT Karlsruhe. If you want a strong research-oriented degree or plan to pursue a PhD later, a TU is ideal for you.

Universities of Applied Sciences (Fachhochschule — FH)

FH universities focus on practical, hands-on education. Programs include mandatory internships and industry projects. They have smaller class sizes, closer ties with local industries, and a more practical curriculum. Examples include Munich University of Applied Sciences and Frankfurt University of Applied Sciences. If you want to start working immediately after your degree, an FH can be a great choice.

Key Differences
  • TU = Research-focused, theoretical, larger class sizes, better for PhD aspirations
  • FH = Industry-focused, practical, mandatory internships, better for direct job placement
  • Both are equally respected by German employers
  • Both offer tuition-free education at public institutions

📋 Admission Requirements

Each university and program may have slightly different requirements, but here are the general admission requirements for Indian students applying to master's programs in Germany.

  • A relevant bachelor's degree (typically 4 years) from a recognised Indian university with a minimum of 60–70% marks or equivalent CGPA
  • English proficiency test — IELTS (6.0–6.5) or TOEFL (80–100) for English-taught programs
  • German language certificate — TestDaF or DSH (B2/C1 level) for German-taught programs
  • Statement of Purpose (SOP) — a well-written essay explaining your motivation and goals
  • Letters of Recommendation (LOR) — typically 2, from professors or employers
  • Updated CV/Resume in the Europass or German format
  • APS certificate (Akademische Prüfstelle) — mandatory for Indian students, verifying your academic credentials
  • GRE/GMAT scores — required by some specific programs (not all)
  • Portfolio or work samples — required for design, architecture, or arts programs

💶 Financial Requirements & Blocked Account

Germany requires international students to prove they can financially support themselves. Here is what you need to know.

Blocked Account (Sperrkonto)

A blocked account is mandatory for your student visa application. You need to deposit approximately €11,208 (as of 2024) into this account before applying for your visa. Once in Germany, you can withdraw about €934 per month to cover your living expenses. Popular blocked account providers include Expatrio, Fintiba, and Deutsche Bank.

Overall Cost Breakdown (Monthly)
  • Rent — €300–€600 (shared accommodation or student dormitory)
  • Food — €200–€300 (cooking at home saves money)
  • Health Insurance — €110–€120 (mandatory for all students)
  • Transport — €30–€60 (semester ticket covers most city travel)
  • Miscellaneous — €100–€150 (phone, internet, personal expenses)
  • Total — approximately €750–€1,200 per month depending on the city
Health Insurance

Health insurance is mandatory in Germany. Public health insurance for students costs approximately €110–€120 per month. Providers like TK (Techniker Krankenkasse), AOK, and Barmer are popular choices among students. You must have health insurance before you can register at your university.

🏆 Scholarships for Indian Students

While tuition is mostly free, living costs can add up. These scholarships can help.

DAAD Scholarships

The German Academic Exchange Service (DAAD) is the largest funding organisation in Germany. It offers various scholarships for Indian students including the DAAD Study Scholarship for master's students (€934/month + travel allowance + health insurance). Applications typically open 12–15 months before the program starts. DAAD also offers short-term research grants and in-country scholarships.

Other Scholarships
  • Erasmus Mundus Joint Master Degrees — fully funded EU scholarships covering tuition, living costs, and travel
  • Heinrich Böll Foundation Scholarship — for students with strong social and political engagement
  • Deutschlandstipendium — €300/month merit-based scholarship offered by individual universities
  • Friedrich Ebert Foundation — for students with outstanding academic records and social commitment
  • SBW Berlin Scholarship — for students from developing countries, covers living and study costs
  • Individual university scholarships — many universities offer their own merit-based scholarships

🎒 Student Life & Part-Time Work Rules

Life as an Indian student in Germany is an exciting mix of academics, work, and European culture.

Part-Time Work
  • You can work 120 full days or 240 half days per year without a work permit
  • Student assistant (HiWi) jobs at universities pay €12–€15 per hour
  • Working student (Werkstudent) positions in companies pay €13–€20 per hour and give industry experience
  • Mini-jobs (up to €520/month) are also popular among students
  • Many students earn €400–€800 per month through part-time work
Student Life
  • German universities have excellent libraries, labs, and sports facilities
  • The Mensa (university canteen) offers meals for €2–€5
  • Student organisations and clubs offer everything from sports to cultural events
  • Indian student communities are active in almost every major German city
  • Public transport is well-connected — most semester tickets cover the entire region
  • Grocery shopping at Aldi, Lidl, and Penny is very affordable

🏡 Permanent Residency (PR) & Settlement After Studies

Germany offers one of the most straightforward paths to permanent residency for international graduates. Here is the typical timeline.

  • Step 1 — Complete your master's degree (2 years typical)
  • Step 2 — Apply for the 18-month job seeker visa (you can work any job during this period to support yourself)
  • Step 3 — Find a job matching your qualification and switch to a work permit or EU Blue Card
  • Step 4 — Work for 21–33 months (with German language skills at B1 level, you can apply for PR in 21 months with EU Blue Card)
  • Step 5 — Apply for permanent settlement permit (Niederlassungserlaubnis)
  • Step 6 — After 6–8 years total in Germany, you can apply for German citizenship if desired

The new Skilled Immigration Act (Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz) has made it even easier for skilled graduates to stay and work in Germany. Germany actively needs skilled professionals in engineering, IT, healthcare, and other sectors — making it a welcoming destination for Indian graduates looking to build long-term careers in Europe.

Frequently Asked Questions

All your questions about studying in Germany — answered

Here are the 15 most common questions Indian students ask us. Click on any question to see the detailed answer.

1. Is education really free in Germany for Indian students? ▼
Yes, most public universities in Germany do not charge tuition fees for master's programs. This applies to all nationalities, including Indian students. You only pay a small semester contribution of €150–350, which usually includes a public transport pass for the semester. The only exception is the state of Baden-Württemberg, which charges €1,500 per semester for non-EU students. However, even this is much cheaper than universities in the US, UK, or Australia.
2. Do I need to learn German to study in Germany? ▼
For English-taught programs, no — you only need IELTS (6.0–6.5) or TOEFL (80–100). There are over 1,600 master's programs in Germany taught entirely in English. However, learning German to at least A2 or B1 level is strongly recommended for daily life. It helps with grocery shopping, dealing with German authorities, understanding documents, making local friends, and getting better part-time jobs. For German-taught programs, you need B2 or C1 level certification through TestDaF or DSH.
3. What is the blocked account and how much money do I need? ▼
A blocked account (Sperrkonto) is a special type of bank account required for your German student visa. It proves that you have enough money to support yourself for one year in Germany. As of 2024, you need to deposit approximately €11,208 (€934 × 12 months). After arriving in Germany, you can withdraw a maximum of €934 per month. You can open a blocked account from India through providers like Expatrio (most popular among Indian students), Fintiba, or Deutsche Bank. The process takes about 1–2 weeks.
4. What is the APS certificate and is it mandatory? ▼
APS stands for Akademische Prüfstelle (Academic Evaluation Centre). It is mandatory for Indian students applying to German universities. The APS certificate verifies that your Indian academic degrees are genuine and equivalent to German standards. The process involves submitting your academic documents to the APS India office in New Delhi, followed by an interview (for some applicants). The entire process can take 4–8 weeks, so start early. Without an APS certificate, most German universities will not process your application.
5. When should I start preparing for my Germany application? ▼
Ideally, start 12–18 months before your intended semester. For a winter semester (October start), begin preparing by January of the previous year. For a summer semester (April start), begin by June–July of the previous year. Your timeline should include: APS certificate (4–8 weeks), language test preparation (2–3 months), university shortlisting and SOP writing (1–2 months), application submission (varies by university), blocked account opening (1–2 weeks), and visa application (6–8 weeks before departure).
6. Can I work while studying in Germany? ▼
Yes! International students in Germany are allowed to work 120 full days or 240 half days per year without needing a separate work permit. Many students work as HiWi (student assistants) at universities, Werkstudent (working students) at companies, or in mini-jobs. The minimum wage in Germany is €12.41 per hour (2024), and many student jobs pay more. Students typically earn €400–€800 per month through part-time work, which can cover a significant portion of living expenses.
7. What are the popular courses for Indian students in Germany? ▼
The most popular master's programs among Indian students include: Computer Science and Data Science, Mechanical Engineering, Electrical Engineering, Automotive Engineering, Business Administration and Management, Information Systems, Robotics and AI, Civil Engineering, Renewable Energy, and Biotechnology. Germany is especially strong in engineering and technology fields, and these graduates have excellent job prospects in the German market.
8. What is the difference between TU and FH universities? ▼
TU (Technische Universität) universities are research-focused with theoretical depth, larger class sizes, and are ideal for students who want to pursue a PhD or career in research. FH (Fachhochschule / University of Applied Sciences) universities are practice-oriented with mandatory internships, smaller classes, and strong industry connections — ideal for students who want to enter the job market immediately after graduation. Both are equally respected by German employers, and both are tuition-free at public institutions.
9. How do I find accommodation in Germany? ▼
Finding accommodation can be challenging, especially in popular cities like Munich and Berlin. Your options include: Studierendenwerk dormitories (cheapest, apply early as waiting lists are long, €200–€400/month), shared apartments called WG (Wohngemeinschaft) found on WG-Gesucht.de (€300–€600/month), and private apartments (€500–€1000+/month). We recommend applying for student dormitories as soon as you receive your admission letter. For WGs, start searching 2–3 months before your arrival.
10. Is health insurance mandatory for students in Germany? ▼
Yes, health insurance is mandatory for all students in Germany. You cannot register at your university without proof of health insurance. Students under 30 years can get public health insurance (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) for approximately €110–€120 per month. Popular providers include TK (Techniker Krankenkasse), AOK, and Barmer. If you are over 30 or in certain situations, you may need private health insurance, which can cost more. We recommend enrolling in public health insurance for the best coverage.
11. What are my chances of getting a scholarship? ▼
Scholarships in Germany are competitive but achievable. The DAAD (German Academic Exchange Service) is the largest provider, offering €934/month plus health insurance and travel costs. Other options include Erasmus Mundus, Deutschlandstipendium (€300/month), and university-specific scholarships. To improve your chances, start applying early (12–15 months before), maintain strong academics (above 8.0 CGPA), have relevant work or research experience, and write a strong motivation letter. Even if you don't get a full scholarship, Germany's low costs make it affordable.
12. What is the visa process for Indian students? ▼
After receiving your admission letter, you apply for a German student visa through VFS Global in India. The process involves: booking a VFS appointment (do this early as slots fill up fast), preparing documents (admission letter, blocked account proof, health insurance, APS certificate, passport, photographs, cover letter), attending the appointment and submitting documents, and waiting for processing (typically 4–8 weeks). The visa fee is €75. You will receive a 3-month entry visa, which you convert to a residence permit after arriving in Germany.
13. Can I bring my family or spouse to Germany on a student visa? ▼
Yes, your spouse can join you in Germany through a family reunion visa (Familienzusammenführung). The requirements include: proof of adequate living space, sufficient financial resources for both of you (higher blocked account amount), valid health insurance for your spouse, and basic German language skills for your spouse (A1 level). Your spouse will also be allowed to work in Germany without restrictions. However, the process can take a few months, so plan accordingly.
14. What happens if my visa gets rejected? ▼
Visa rejections are rare if your documents are in order, but they do happen. Common reasons include: insufficient financial proof, incomplete documents, unconvincing motivation, or inconsistencies in your application. If your visa is rejected, you can file a remonstrance (formal appeal) within one month, addressing the reasons for rejection. Many successful appeals result in visa approval. We help our students prepare strong applications to minimize rejection risks and assist with remonstrance if needed.
15. Can I get permanent residency (PR) in Germany after my studies? ▼
Yes, Germany offers a very clear path to PR for international graduates. After completing your degree, you get an 18-month job seeker visa. Once you find a job matching your qualification, you switch to a work permit or EU Blue Card. With an EU Blue Card and B1 German language skills, you can apply for permanent residency (Niederlassungserlaubnis) after just 21 months of working. Without B1 German, it takes 33 months. After 6–8 years in Germany, you can also apply for German citizenship. This is one of the most attractive aspects of studying in Germany compared to other countries.
